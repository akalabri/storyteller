Scene One — The First Footprints.
In a forest buried under deep snow, a small fox with copper-red fur and curious eyes stepped out from beneath a pine. The world was silent except for the crunch of her paws. She had no name yet, only hunger and cold — and then she saw it: a key, half buried in the ice, that caught the light like a tiny star. She nosed it free and held it. Something in her chest flickered. This key was meant for somewhere.

Scene Two — The Hollow Tree.
The fox followed a path only she could feel, past frozen streams and sleeping birds, until she reached a hollow tree. The key fit the lock hidden in the bark. Inside, the walls glowed with the same soft light as the key. There were drawings there — other foxes, other winters — and a single sentence carved in the wood: "Who finds the key finds the way home."

Scene Three — The Other Side of the Forest.
She left the hollow and walked on, key around her neck. The forest began to change. Snow gave way to patches of green; the air grew warmer. She was no longer only surviving. She was looking for the door that the key would open — and for whoever had left the drawings in the tree.

Scene Four — The Cottage in the Clearing.
At the edge of the forest stood a small cottage, smoke rising from the chimney. In the window was a lamp, and beside it, another fox — older, with a scarf and kind eyes — was looking out at the snow. When she saw the small fox with the key, she went still. Then she opened the door. No words were needed. The key had brought the little fox home.

Scene Five — The Fire and the Future.
By the fire, the two foxes sat. The little one told her story in looks and small sounds; the older one listened and nodded. Outside, the snow kept falling, but inside there was warmth and the promise of spring. The fox was no longer alone. She had a name now, given in that moment: Ember — for the light she had carried through the winter.
